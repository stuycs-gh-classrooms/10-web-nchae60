When graphed against the reciprocal of the numeric concentration, a second order reaction will linearize. As stated prior, if these reactions are graphed against numeric concentration, the second and first order appear far too similar to distinguish reliably.

Akin to the first-order reaction, explaining linearization involves calculus, and the mathematical explanation is that integrating the differential equation leads to a linear equation in which the reciprocal concentration happens to be the y and b terms. The major difference, however, appears once the graph is linearized. While the first and zero order reactions linearize with a negative slope, the second order reaction linearizes with a positive slope, as the progress of the reaction causes the denominator of the function to decrease.
